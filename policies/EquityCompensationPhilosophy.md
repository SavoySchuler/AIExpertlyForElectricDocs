# AIExpertly Equity & Compensation Philosophy

Version: v0.2 draft  
Status: Founder review draft

> This document explains how AIExpertly thinks about ownership, compensation, incentives, dilution, founder economics, employee equity, advisors, and future compensation decisions.

## 1. Purpose

This document gives founders and future leaders a consistent way to think about ownership, salaries, bonuses, founder economics, equity grants, advisor grants, dilution, and incentives.

It is not a grant document, tax document, or securities-law analysis. Specific grants require proper approval and legal/tax review.

## 2. Core Philosophy

Ownership reflects long-term risk and partnership. Compensation reflects current contribution. Equity should be granted deliberately, documented carefully, and used to align long-term incentives.

The Company should avoid using equity as a substitute for unclear expectations.

## 3. Founder Ownership

The initial three founders hold equal ownership and voting power, subject to reverse vesting. Founder ownership should not be renegotiated solely because one founder works more hours in a particular period. Differences in current contribution should be addressed through salary, bonus, support, role adjustment, or future grants rather than constant cap table renegotiation.

## 4. Reverse Vesting

Founder units are issued immediately but subject to Company repurchase rights over a four-year vesting schedule with a one-year cliff and monthly vesting thereafter. This protects the Company from the dead-founder problem while allowing founders to be true owners from day one.

## 5. Compensation Principles

Compensation should be financially sustainable, role-based, market-aware where possible, transparent between founders, reviewed regularly, separate from ownership, and approved under the Governance Matrix.

No founder should unilaterally approve their own compensation.

## 6. Salary Before Profit Distributions

Salaries compensate labor. Distributions allocate profits. The Company should avoid using distributions as a substitute for fair salary when founders have materially different roles or time commitments.

## 7. Profit Distributions

Profits and losses are allocated by ownership percentage. Tax distributions should be made when possible to prevent phantom income. Excess Cash is presumed distributable under the Operating Agreement, but the Company should retain capital needed for operations, reserves, growth, hiring, debt service, and approved strategic opportunities.

## 8. Founder Loans

Founder loans should be documented, standardized, and kept separate from ownership unless the Members approve an equity transaction. No handshake loans. No automatic conversion to equity.

Standard founder loan terms should address principal amount, purpose, interest rate, repayment trigger, subordination, maturity, whether prepayment is allowed, and approval record.

## 9. Equity Incentive Plan

The Company may create an equity incentive plan of up to 10% of fully diluted ownership with required approval. The pool should not be created before there is a real business need. The Company may use LLC profits interests, restricted units, phantom equity, or, after conversion, stock options or restricted stock.

## 10. Employee Equity

Employee equity should be based on role seniority, timing and risk, expected impact, market compensation gap, retention importance, dilution budget, vesting period, and current company stage.

Early employees may receive larger grants because they accept more risk and ambiguity. Later employees may receive smaller grants because the Company is more de-risked.

## 11. Advisor Equity

Advisor equity should be used sparingly and tied to specific outcomes. Advisor grants should include advisor role, expected contribution, vesting or milestone schedule, confidentiality obligations, IP assignment if applicable, and termination rights.

Advisor grants should be reviewed at least annually for continued value.

## 12. Contractor Equity

Contractor equity should be rare. Contractors should generally be paid cash. Equity may be appropriate when the contractor provides strategic, difficult-to-replace, company-building value beyond ordinary services.

## 13. Dilution Philosophy

Dilution is acceptable when it increases expected enterprise value more than it reduces ownership percentage. The Company should disclose all equity issuances with a pro forma cap table, valuation assumptions, rights granted, and resulting dilution.

Good dilution buys capability, capital, credibility, or distribution that the Company could not otherwise obtain on reasonable terms.

## 14. Preemptive Rights

Founders should have preemptive rights to preserve proportional ownership when new equity is issued, subject to exceptions approved under the Operating Agreement.

## 15. Bonuses

Bonuses should be tied to measurable business outcomes where possible. Bonus criteria may include revenue, profitability, launch milestones, customer retention, fundraising success, operational improvements, reliability, security, or strategic execution.

## 16. Promotions and Role Changes

Promotion may affect salary, bonus, title, authority, and future grants. Promotion should not automatically affect founder ownership or already-granted equity except under the applicable grant documents.

## 17. Founder Compensation Review

Founder compensation should be reviewed at least annually and during any material change in workload, officer responsibilities, profitability, fundraising status, market compensation data, Company cash position, personal guarantees, or founder loans.

The review should distinguish fairness to the individual from sustainability for the Company.

## 18. Leave, Burnout, and Reduced Capacity

Approved Leave, sabbatical, reduced schedule, or temporary capacity changes should generally affect role expectations and compensation before affecting ownership. If leave may exceed six months, the founders should document the expected effect on responsibilities, compensation, and vesting.

## 19. Compensation Review Cadence

The Company should review compensation at least annually and whenever there is a material change in role, funding, profitability, market conditions, workload, or risk.

## 20. Compensation Records

The Company should maintain written records for founder salaries, employee compensation, bonuses, equity grants, advisor grants, contractor grants, board or member approvals, cap table updates, valuation support, tax review notes, and grant documents.

## 21. Equity Grant Checklist

Before approving an equity grant, confirm:

1. Is there an approved equity plan or specific approval authority?
2. What instrument will be used?
3. What is the vesting schedule?
4. What service expectations apply?
5. What IP and confidentiality obligations apply?
6. What happens if service terminates?
7. What is the dilution impact?
8. Is tax or securities-law review needed?
9. Has the Cap Table been updated?
10. Can this decision be explained to future investors or acquirers?

## 22. Guiding Test

Before making an equity or compensation decision, ask:

1. Does this align incentives with long-term enterprise value?
2. Is the dilution justified?
3. Is the decision fair relative to role, risk, and contribution?
4. Can we explain this decision to future employees, investors, and attorneys?
5. Is the decision documented clearly enough to avoid future disputes?
