# AI Expertly for Electric Founders Charter

Version: v0.2 draft  
Status: Founder review draft

> The Founders Charter is a non-legal companion to the Operating Agreement. It is intended to create alignment, not replace attorney-reviewed legal documents.

## 1. Purpose

The Founders Charter is the cultural and strategic constitution of AI Expertly for Electric. It explains how the founders intend to build, govern, disagree, decide, repair, and evolve the Company.

The Operating Agreement defines enforceable rights. This Charter defines the partnership norms that help those rights work in practice.

## 2. Founding Intent

AI Expertly for Electric exists to build durable AI-enabled products and services that create meaningful value for customers, employees, founders, and future stakeholders. The founders intend to build a company that can grow beyond its original products and beyond the direct daily involvement of the original founders.

The Company should be designed to survive success: growth, profit, hiring, fundraising, burnout, leadership changes, acquisition offers, and strategic pivots.

## 3. Company Principles

1. Long-term enterprise value over short-term extraction.
2. Company continuity over individual convenience.
3. Candor over avoidance.
4. Good-faith disagreement over silent resentment.
5. Clear ownership, clear roles, clear accountability.
6. Technical excellence in service of customer value.
7. Customer trust as a strategic asset.
8. Responsible AI by design, not as an afterthought.
9. Documentation as operating leverage.
10. Governance as a product that should improve over time.
11. Compensation should reflect current contribution; ownership should reflect long-term partnership.
12. Decisions should be made at the lowest responsible level unless they materially change Company risk.

## 4. Founder Relationship Principles

The founders agree that equal ownership does not mean identical responsibilities. The Company should preserve equal founding partnership while assigning clear operational accountability.

Founders should:

- raise concerns early;
- avoid surprise escalations;
- disclose material information promptly;
- assume good faith unless evidence shows otherwise;
- document major decisions;
- separate disagreement about an idea from judgment of a person;
- distinguish ownership, employment, compensation, and officer authority;
- treat the other founders as long-term partners even during conflict;
- explain objections to Reserved Matters with business reasons;
- use formal dispute processes only when direct repair fails.

## 5. Decision Philosophy

AI Expertly for Electric should not require founder votes for ordinary execution. Authority should follow expertise, while accountability follows governance.

- The CEO owns business execution and enterprise value.
- The CTO owns technical architecture and engineering execution.
- The CPO owns product value and roadmap clarity.
- The CAIO owns AI practices, evaluation, safety, and AI governance within the technology organization.
- The COO owns operational scale and process reliability.
- The CFO owns financial discipline.
- The CIO owns internal systems and enterprise IT.
- The CXO owns customer experience and retention.
- The CBO owns partnerships and strategic growth.

Strategic decisions should be escalated when they materially affect ownership, financial risk, company strategy, long-term technology commitments, customer trust, IP ownership, or regulatory exposure.

## 6. Disagreement Norms

The founders expect disagreement. Disagreement is not failure. Avoided disagreement is failure.

When disagreement arises, the founders should:

1. State the business question clearly.
2. Identify the decision owner.
3. Identify the approval threshold.
4. Share relevant facts and assumptions.
5. Explain the business rationale for positions on Reserved Matters.
6. Identify whether the decision is reversible.
7. Identify the cost of delay.
8. Seek a solution that preserves company continuity.
9. Use the formal dispute process only after good-faith effort.

## 7. Repair Norms

When a disagreement damages trust, the founders should not pretend the only choices are immediate normalcy or formal dispute escalation. A lightweight repair conversation should address:

- what happened;
- what each founder understood at the time;
- what was missed or withheld;
- what the Company needs now;
- what will be done differently next time;
- whether a written decision record or schedule update is needed.

Repair is not about assigning blame. It is about restoring operating clarity.

## 8. Communication Cadence

The founders should maintain:

- Weekly founder operating meeting.
- Monthly financial and product review.
- Quarterly Member meeting.
- Semi-annual founder performance and expectations review.
- Annual strategic planning meeting.
- Annual governance review.

Meetings should produce clear decisions, owners, dates, and follow-ups.

## 9. Founder Performance Philosophy

Founder performance should be addressed directly, but vested founder equity should not be weaponized for subjective performance disputes. Performance concerns should affect role, support, compensation, and future grants before they affect ownership. Only objective Bad Leaver events, abandonment, or agreed buy-sell processes should affect vested ownership.

## 10. Compensation Philosophy

Ownership is durable. Compensation is adaptive.

If one founder contributes more time, takes on more operational burden, or assumes a more demanding role, the Company should address that through salary, bonus, role allocation, support, or future equity grants rather than repeatedly reopening founder ownership.

## 11. AI Principles

AI Expertly for Electric should treat AI capability, customer trust, data rights, and model provenance as core assets.

The Company should:

- maintain clear model and data provenance;
- avoid unauthorized customer data training;
- document major AI architecture decisions;
- evaluate model quality, safety, reliability, and cost;
- preserve flexibility to change providers and tools;
- escalate strategic AI commitments that materially change business risk;
- treat prompt libraries, workflows, evaluations, and AI operating knowledge as Company IP.

## 12. Customer Philosophy

Customer trust should be protected even when short-term revenue creates pressure. The Company should avoid commitments it cannot meet, disclose material limitations where appropriate, and treat customer data rights as a contractual and reputational obligation.

## 13. Documentation Philosophy

Major decisions should be recorded in a Decision Journal. The journal should capture:

- decision;
- date;
- approving parties;
- rationale;
- alternatives considered;
- expected outcome;
- review date, where applicable.

The goal is not bureaucracy. The goal is institutional memory.

## 14. Funding and Risk Philosophy

The founders should explicitly review their posture toward:

- bootstrapping versus outside capital;
- profitability versus growth;
- venture-scale outcomes versus durable cash-flow business;
- customer concentration risk;
- technical risk;
- regulated-market risk;
- acquisition timing.

Misalignment on funding and risk should be treated as a strategic issue, not a personality conflict.

## 15. Workload and Life Change Philosophy

The founders recognize that life changes happen: illness, caregiving, parental leave, burnout, relocation, and changing capacity. The default response should be role clarity, coverage, and compensation adjustment rather than immediate ownership renegotiation.

Approved Leave should be handled transparently and documented before ambiguity becomes resentment.

## 16. Annual Recommitment

Once each year, the founders should review this Charter and answer:

1. Are we still building the same kind of company?
2. Are our roles still accurate?
3. Are our work expectations still fair?
4. Are we aligned on funding, profitability, and risk?
5. Are we resolving disagreements quickly enough?
6. Are we protecting customer trust and company continuity?
7. Are our governance documents still useful?
8. Are there assumptions we need to make explicit?

Updates to this Charter should be documented but do not amend the Operating Agreement unless expressly stated.
