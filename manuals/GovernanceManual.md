# AI Expertly Vehicle Imports Governance Manual

Version: v0.2 draft  
Status: Founder review draft

> This manual explains how to operate the governance system. It is a practical companion to the Operating Agreement, not a substitute for legal review.

## 1. Purpose

The Governance Manual explains how AI Expertly Vehicle Imports operates the framework established in the Operating Agreement. It is intended to make governance practical, repeatable, and easy for founders, officers, and future executives to use.

The goal is to prevent avoidable ambiguity. The legal agreement defines authority. This manual explains how to use that authority without slowing the business down.

## 2. Governance Architecture

AI Expertly Vehicle Imports uses four layers of governance:

1. **Operating Agreement** — binding legal foundation.
2. **Governance Matrix** — approval thresholds and decision rights.
3. **Schedules and Handbooks** — role definitions, expectations, and workflows.
4. **Policies and Playbooks** — practical implementation guidance.

The core legal agreement should remain stable. Operational details should evolve through schedules and policies.

## 3. Governance Principles

AI Expertly Vehicle Imports' governance system is designed around these principles:

- Ownership is equal at formation, but operational accountability is role-based.
- The CEO is accountable for company execution.
- The CTO owns technical architecture and implementation.
- The CPO owns product direction and customer value.
- The CAIO owns AI governance and model evaluation within the technology organization.
- Member votes should govern business risk, ownership, capital structure, and strategic direction, not routine implementation details.
- Silence is never approval.
- Major decisions should be documented with rationale, alternatives, and expected outcomes.

## 4. Decision Categories

### 4.1 Domain Decisions

Domain decisions are made by the responsible executive within approved budgets and plans. Examples include engineering tools, ordinary vendor selection, roadmap execution, customer support workflows, and internal processes.

### 4.2 Cross-Functional Decisions

A cross-functional decision materially affects more than one executive domain. The responsible executive should consult affected executives before acting. If executives cannot agree, the CEO decides unless the matter is a Strategic Company Decision.

### 4.3 Strategic Company Decisions

Strategic Company Decisions materially affect ownership, voting, control, debt, acquisition, financing, regulated market exposure, sale or exclusive license of core IP, customer data rights, or long-term technology commitments. These decisions follow the Governance Matrix.

### 4.4 Constitutional Decisions

Constitutional decisions change the founders' bargain. These include ownership percentages, voting rights, vesting terms, buy-sell rights, preemptive rights, deadlock procedures, and amendment of the Operating Agreement. These generally require Unanimous Approval.

## 6. Notice and Voting Procedures

### 6.1 Notice Periods

| Action | Minimum Notice |
|---|---:|
| Regular Member meeting | 14 calendar days |
| Special Member meeting | 5 business days |
| Emergency meeting | As much notice as reasonably practical |
| Reserved Matter vote | 14 calendar days unless emergency |
| Acquisition, financing, sale, conversion | 30 calendar days unless external timing requires faster action |

### 6.2 Voting Windows

| Matter | Voting Window |
|---|---:|
| Routine Member matter | 7 calendar days |
| Reserved Matter | 14 calendar days |
| Strategic transaction | 30 calendar days unless the opportunity expires sooner |

### 6.3 Silence and Abstentions

Silence is not approval. Abstentions are not approval unless expressly documented as such in the approval record. If a Member does not vote, the action is not approved unless the required approval threshold is otherwise satisfied.

### 6.4 Electronic Voting

Votes may be collected through email, electronic signature platform, secure workspace, board portal, or archived collaboration tool. The approval record should identify:

- action approved;
- approval threshold;
- Members voting;
- date and time;
- final decision;
- documents reviewed.

### 6.5 Written Materials for Reserved Matters

For Reserved Matters, the proposer should circulate:

- proposal summary;
- business rationale;
- financial impact;
- risks;
- alternatives considered;
- relevant documents;
- approval threshold;
- voting deadline.

## 7. Decision Records

### 7.1 Decision Journal

Major governance decisions should be recorded in the Decision Journal. The record should include:

- decision;
- date;
- approving Members;
- rationale;
- alternatives considered;
- expected outcome;
- review date;
- links to supporting documents.

### 7.2 Why Decision Records Matter

Decision records reduce future disputes because they preserve the reasoning behind choices. Five years later, the Company should know not only what it decided, but why.

## 8. Annual Strategic Planning Process

The Annual Strategic Plan should answer:

1. What kind of company are we building?
2. What customers are we prioritizing?
3. What products or services matter most this year?
4. What risks are we willing to take?
5. What risks are we unwilling to take?
6. What is our financing posture: bootstrap, angels, venture, debt, or strategic partnership?
7. What are the major technical bets?
8. What are the major customer and revenue bets?

### Outputs

- Annual Strategic Plan.
- Annual Operating Plan.
- Budget.
- Hiring plan.
- Capital allocation plan.
- Target operating reserve.
- Strategic Technology Decision review, if needed.

## 9. Founder Review Process

Founder Reviews happen every six months. They should be structured, written, and non-punitive.

### Review Agenda

1. Current responsibilities.
2. Progress against goals.
3. Time commitment and availability.
4. Areas of overload or under-support.
5. Performance concerns.
6. Compensation review.
7. Role or title adjustments.
8. Updates to Founder Roles Schedule.
9. Support needed from the other Founders or Company.

### Guardrail

Founder Review outcomes may affect officer roles, compensation, support, and future equity grants. They should not affect vested founder equity unless conduct independently qualifies as Cause, abandonment, or another Bad Leaver event.

## 10. Handling Disagreement Before Deadlock

Before invoking formal dispute resolution, founders should complete a structured disagreement review:

1. State the decision in one sentence.
2. Identify the approval threshold.
3. Identify the business objective.
4. List each founder's position.
5. Identify shared facts and disputed facts.
6. Identify reversible versus irreversible consequences.
7. Identify the cost of delay.
8. Identify a limited experiment or reversible path, if possible.
9. Decide whether the issue is operational, strategic, or constitutional.

If unresolved, follow the Operating Agreement's negotiation, mediation, arbitration, and deadlock procedures.

## 11. Strategic Technology Decisions

The Company should not vote on routine engineering implementation. The CTO should decide ordinary technical choices within budget.

### CTO-Decided Examples

- Commercial LLM provider choice.
- Vector database.
- Model routing.
- Prompt orchestration.
- Frameworks and programming languages.
- Cloud tooling.
- CI/CD and monitoring.

### Governance-Level Examples

- Building a proprietary foundation model.
- Selling or exclusively licensing Core Technology.
- Releasing proprietary model weights.
- Selling strategic datasets.
- Entering regulated AI markets.
- Major infrastructure commitments above approved thresholds.
- Material changes to customer data rights.

## 12. Acquisition or Financing Review

When a bona fide acquisition or financing opportunity arises:

1. CEO discloses the opportunity to all Members within five business days.
2. All material documents are shared subject to confidentiality.
3. Members receive at least thirty days to evaluate unless external timing requires faster action.
4. The Company prepares a transaction memo covering valuation, dilution, control, obligations, legal risk, tax risk, employee impact, customer impact, and alternatives.
5. The Decision Journal records the final decision.

## 13. Governance Review

At least annually, the Members should review:

- Governance Matrix thresholds.
- Spending limits.
- Officer roles.
- Founder expectations.
- AI Governance Policy.
- Security Policy.
- Equity incentive needs.
- Insurance needs.
- Key-person insurance or buy-sell funding.
- Whether the Company should remain a Washington LLC.

## 14. Amendment Philosophy

The Company should minimize amendments to the core Operating Agreement. Durable legal rules belong in the Agreement. Frequently changing operational details belong in schedules or policies.

## 15. Attorney Review Checklist

Before signing or relying on the Operating Agreement, counsel should review:

- Washington LLC enforceability.
- Reverse vesting and repurchase mechanics.
- Good Leaver / Bad Leaver provisions.
- Non-solicit enforceability.
- Tax distribution language.
- Profits interests or future equity incentive structure.
- Spousal consent language.
- Arbitration and shotgun clause enforceability.
- Data, AI, IP, and customer-contract implications.
- Securities law implications for future equity grants.
